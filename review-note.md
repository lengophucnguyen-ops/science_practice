## Chapter 1: Photosynthesis and Carbon Cycle

**Key Ideas:**

*   **Photosynthesis:** The process by which green plants and some other organisms use sunlight to synthesize foods from carbon dioxide and water. It primarily occurs in the leaves.
    *   **Equation:** Carbon Dioxide + Water + Light Energy → Glucose + Oxygen
*   **Chloroplast:** Organelles found in plant cells that conduct photosynthesis. They contain chlorophyll.
*   **Chlorophyll:** The green pigment in plants that absorbs sunlight for photosynthesis.
*   **Respiration:** The process where organisms break down glucose to release energy for their life processes. It occurs in all living cells.
    *   **Equation:** Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon Dioxide + Water + Energy
*   **Combustion:** The process of burning something, especially fossil fuels, which releases carbon dioxide into the atmosphere.
*   **Carbon Cycle:** The natural process by which carbon atoms continually travel from the atmosphere to the Earth and then back into the atmosphere. It involves photosynthesis, respiration, decomposition, and combustion.
*   **Climate Change:** Long-term shifts in temperatures and weather patterns, largely caused by increased levels of greenhouse gases (like carbon dioxide from human activities) in the atmosphere, which trap heat.

## Chapter 2: Properties of Material

**Key Ideas:**

*   **Periodic Table:** A chart that organizes all known chemical elements by their atomic number, electron configurations, and recurring chemical properties.
*   **Atom:** The basic unit of matter, consisting of a dense central nucleus surrounded by a cloud of negatively charged electrons.
*   **Electron, Proton, Neutron:**
    *   **Proton:** Positively charged particle found in the nucleus. (Relative mass: 1, Charge: +1)
    *   **Neutron:** Neutral particle found in the nucleus. (Relative mass: 1, Charge: 0)
    *   **Electron:** Negatively charged particle orbiting the nucleus in shells. (Relative mass: negligible, Charge: -1)
*   **Non-metal and Metal:**
    *   **Metals:** Generally shiny, good conductors of heat and electricity, malleable (can be hammered into sheets), ductile (can be drawn into wires), high melting points. (e.g., Iron, Copper)
    *   **Non-metals:** Generally dull, poor conductors of heat and electricity (insulators), brittle, lower melting points. (e.g., Oxygen, Carbon)
*   **Electron Layers (Shells):** Energy levels around the nucleus where electrons are found. Atoms tend to react to achieve a stable outer electron shell (full outer shell).
*   **Melting Point:** The temperature at which a solid turns into a liquid.
*   **Ionic Bonding:** Chemical bond formed between a metal and a non-metal where electrons are transferred from the metal atom to the non-metal atom, forming oppositely charged ions that attract each other. (e.g., Sodium Chloride, NaCl)
*   **Covalent Bonding:** Chemical bond formed between two non-metal atoms where electrons are shared between them to achieve a stable outer shell. (e.g., Water, H₂O)
*   **Simple Structure (Simple Molecular):** Substances with small molecules held together by weak intermolecular forces. They have low melting and boiling points and do not conduct electricity. (e.g., Water, Carbon Dioxide)
*   **Giant Structure:**
    *   **Giant Covalent:** Atoms held together by strong covalent bonds in a large, continuous network. Very high melting points, hard, do not conduct electricity (except graphite). (e.g., Diamond, Silicon Dioxide)
    *   **Giant Ionic Lattice:** A regular arrangement of positive and negative ions held together by strong electrostatic forces. High melting points, conduct electricity when molten or dissolved. (e.g., Sodium Chloride)
    *   **Metallic Structure:** A lattice of positive metal ions surrounded by a 'sea' of delocalized electrons. Good conductors of heat and electricity, malleable, ductile, high melting points.

## Chapter 3: Force and Energy

**Key Ideas:**

*   **Density:** A measure of how much mass is contained in a given volume.
    *   **Formula:** Density = Mass / Volume
*   **Mass:** The amount of matter in an object (measured in kilograms, kg).
*   **Volume:** The amount of space an object occupies (measured in cubic meters, m³, or cubic centimeters, cm³).
*   **State of Matter:** The physical forms in which matter can exist:
    *   **Solid:** Fixed shape and volume, particles are tightly packed and vibrate in fixed positions.
    *   **Liquid:** Fixed volume but takes the shape of its container, particles are close but can move past each other.
    *   **Gas:** No fixed shape or volume, particles are far apart and move randomly and rapidly.
*   **Heat:** A form of energy that is transferred from a hotter object to a colder object.
*   **Temperature:** A measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance, indicating how hot or cold something is.
*   **Work Done:** The energy transferred when a force moves an object over a distance.
    *   **Formula:** Work Done = Force × Distance (W = Fd)
*   **Conservation of Energy:** Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred from one form to another.
*   **Radiation (Heat Transfer):** Transfer of heat through electromagnetic waves (e.g., heat from the sun, heat from a fire). Does not require a medium.
*   **Convection (Heat Transfer):** Transfer of heat through the movement of fluids (liquids or gases). Hotter, less dense fluid rises, and colder, denser fluid sinks, creating a convection current.
*   **Conduction (Heat Transfer):** Transfer of heat through direct contact between particles, typically in solids. Particles vibrate and pass energy to adjacent particles.
*   **Cooling:** The process of losing heat, resulting in a decrease in temperature.
*   **Evaporation:** The process by which a liquid turns into a gas at a temperature below its boiling point.
*   **Melting:** The process by which a solid turns into a liquid when heated to its melting point.

## Chapter 4: Maintaining Life

**Key Ideas:**

*   **Plant:** Living organisms that typically produce their own food through photosynthesis and are essential for most ecosystems.
*   **Water:** Crucial for plant survival (photosynthesis, transport, turgidity) and for all life processes in animals.
*   **Root Hair Cell:** Specialized plant cells in the roots that have a large surface area to absorb water and mineral ions from the soil efficiently.
*   **Cells:** The basic structural and functional unit of all known organisms.
*   **Xylem:** Plant tissue that transports water and dissolved minerals from the roots up to the rest of the plant.
*   **Osmosis:** The net movement of water molecules from a region of higher water potential to a region of lower water potential across a partially permeable membrane. Important for water uptake in plants.
*   **Transpiration:** The process by which water vapor is released from plants, primarily through the stomata in their leaves, into the atmosphere.
*   **Plant Structure:** Key parts include roots (anchor, absorb water), stem (support, transport), and leaves (photosynthesis).
*   **Excretion:** The process of removing metabolic waste products and excess substances from the body.
*   **Urea:** A waste product formed in the liver from the breakdown of excess amino acids, excreted by the kidneys.
*   **Kidneys:** Organs in the excretory system that filter blood to remove waste products (like urea) and excess water, forming urine.
*   **Fetus:** An unborn offspring of a mammal, in particular an unborn human baby more than eight weeks after conception.
*   **Drugs:** Substances that affect the body's functions, often used for medicinal purposes but can also be abused.
*   **Pregnancy:** The period during which a fetus develops inside a woman's womb.

## Chapter 5: Reactivity

**Key Ideas:**

*   **Displacement Reaction:** A chemical reaction in which a more reactive element takes the place of a less reactive element in a compound.
*   **Reaction (Chemical Reaction):** A process that involves rearrangement of the structure of molecules or ions, resulting in the formation of new substances.
*   **Reactivity Series Table:** A list of metals (and sometimes non-metals) arranged in order of their decreasing reactivity. More reactive metals can displace less reactive metals from their compounds.
*   **Salts:** Ionic compounds formed when an acid reacts with a base, where the hydrogen ion of the acid is replaced by a metal ion or ammonium ion. (e.g., NaCl, CuSO₄)
    *   **Formula for Salt Creation (Neutralization):** Acid + Base → Salt + Water
    *   **Other Salt Creation Reactions:**
        *   **Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen Gas** (e.g., 2HCl + Mg → MgCl₂ + H₂)
        *   **Acid + Carbonate → Salt + Water + Carbon Dioxide** (e.g., 2HCl + CaCO₃ → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂)
        *   **Acid + Metal Oxide → Salt + Water** (e.g., 2HCl + CuO → CuCl₂ + H₂O)
*   **Acids:** Substances that produce hydrogen ions (H⁺) when dissolved in water. They have a pH less than 7, taste sour, and react with metals, bases, and carbonates. (e.g., Hydrochloric acid, HCl)
*   **Base:** A substance that neutralizes an acid. They are often metal oxides or hydroxides.
*   **Alkalis:** Soluble bases that produce hydroxide ions (OH⁻) when dissolved in water. They have a pH greater than 7, feel soapy, and are corrosive. (e.g., Sodium hydroxide, NaOH)
*   **Rearranging Atoms:** During a chemical reaction, atoms are not created or destroyed, but rather rearranged to form new molecules.
*   **Conservation of Mass:** In any closed system, the mass of the reactants in a chemical reaction must equal the mass of the products.

## Chapter 6 (only 6.1): Sound and Spaces

**Key Ideas:**

*   **Amplitude:** The maximum displacement or distance moved by a point on a vibrating body or wave measured from its equilibrium position. In sound, larger amplitude means louder sound.
*   **Frequency:** The number of complete waves or vibrations that pass a point in a given amount of time. In sound, higher frequency means higher pitch.
    *   **Unit:** Hertz (Hz)
*   **Peak (Crest):** The highest point of a wave.
*   **Trough:** The lowest point of a wave.
*   **Pitch:** How high or low a sound is, determined by its frequency.
*   **Loudness:** The perception of the intensity of a sound, determined by its amplitude.
*   **Oscilloscope:** An electronic instrument that displays varying signal voltages, often used to visualize sound waves and measure their amplitude and frequency.
*   **Hertz (Hz):** The SI unit of frequency, equal to one cycle per second.
*   **Vibration:** A rapid back-and-forth movement. Sound is produced by vibrations that create waves in a medium (like air).
